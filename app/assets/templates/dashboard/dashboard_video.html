Disclaimer  

This process is designed as an internal, self-assessment tool to help corporate sustainability teams and their advisors uncover and prioritize opportunities to advance sustainability integration and therefore the potential to create more value for their companies and other stakeholders.  It is not intended to be used an external rating system or reporting tool.  Through its electronic form, the UN Global Compact will capture and aggregate numeric data and comments to improve its tools and resources.  It may on occasion disclose aggregate data from multiple companies and cite example quotations.  The Global Compact will not attribute any data or quotations to any company without their expressed, written approval.

Introduction

Integrating Sustainability into Business Functions

Increasingly companies with experience in corporate sustainability conclude that deeper integration into their business functions and management processes is necessary to achieve greater levels of value creation, especially when shifting the focus of sustainability beyond risk management and productivity to innovating for growth (see Sustainability Stage Model in the Readiness Section). Even the best sustainability teams can only take their companies so far.

 

In traditional business terms, organizational integration applies to efforts that seek to continuously align the strategies, practices and policies of autonomous and independent entities to facilitate coordination, collaboration, synergies and greater innovation.   

 

The Roadmap for Integrated Sustainability project seeks to help sustainability teams, or executive leaders responsible for sustainability goals, to engage and integrate sustainability with the company’s priority business functions. The choice of priority functions and the engagement strategies differ depending on the developmental stage of sustainability and its integration in overall business strategy.

 

The Self-Assessment tool is organized in four sections:

  1. Introduction and Instructions

  2. Readiness - 5 questions related to the relative development and overall integration of sustainability at the corporate level.

  3. Priority Functions - A quick, subjective survey to help identify priority functions for deeper analysis in section 4.

  4. Function specific sets of questions and comments - These questions are designed to be answered through direct discussions between sustainability leaders and peers in the chosen function.

The Roadmap Sustainability Stages Model

Though every company’s sustainability journey is unique, many companies evolve through similar stages with similar characteristics, priorities and opportunities to create value.

Underpinning the Roadmap for Integrated Sustainability project, and in particular the Roadmap Self-Assessment tool, is a Sustainability Stages model, which represents a typical journey of sustainability integration in five general stages. Progressing through the stages, the model describes how companies generally move from a mostly reactive approach to sustainability aimed at protecting the brand and reducing risks to a more proactive and strategic approach, where sustainability becomes a source of resource optimization and value creation.

  1. Stage 1 - Crisis Management - The company’s license to operate is threatened by legal enforcement actions or public outcry.

  2. Stage 2 - Compliance - The company manages its liabilities by ensuring compliance with the law and all labour, environmental, health and safety regulations.

  3. Stage 3 - Resource Optimization - The company moves beyond compliance and risk to reducing costs and increasing productivity.

  4. Stage 4 - Market Differentiation - The company shifts to sustainability-driven innovation, differentiation and growth.

  5. Stage 5 - Purpose-Driven - In this, the highest stage of sustainability integration currently imagined, sustainability issues have become inseparable from the core vision, mission and brand of the company.

The 3 Lenses of Integrated Sustainability

The process of engagement and integration is characterized in three separate but interrelated facets: Strategic, Operational and Cultural Integration.  

  • Strategic Integration - Incorporating sustainability into the core strategy of an organization (enterprise, business unit, or function). In this context, “strategy” is used to describe the deliberate or emergent choices (purpose, priorities, and goals) that are made to distinctively position the enterprise, brand or product for “sustainable” (enduring) competitive advantage and value creation. It’s about “Doing the right things.”

  • Operational Integration - Executing and reviewing the performance of sustainability related strategies in the regular routine of an organization.  This lens describes the processes, policies and practices put in place to improve the execution of strategy or sometimes called operational effectiveness. It’s about “Doing things right.”  

  • Cultural Integration - Leveraging the unique identity, culture, purpose and strengths of an organization to advance sustainability and corporate success. To achieve a business’s potential, strategic and operational integration must consider the unique culture, identity and strengths of organizations and their components. Culture can be a significant barrier or powerful amplifier of executing strategy.

Assessment Tool Process:

  1. The tool is designed to be lead by the Sustainability Function completing Sections 1-3  by itself (no more than 30 minutes) and Section 4 in dialog with functional peers (approximately 30-45 minutes each).  For each question in Sections 2 and 4, please indicate your best assessment of your sustainability program on three dimensions:

  1. Current Performance: For each question, pick the level of performance (1 to 5) that best characterizes your current state.  While you will likely have examples of programs described by multiple answers, choose the selection that describes the “center of gravity” of your current program.

  2. Target Performance: Indicate the level of performance you would like to achieve (same scale, 1 to 5)

  3. Criticality: Indicate how critical this capability is to driving business value through sustainability (Low, Medium, or High)

  4. Comments are optional, but encouraged to capture new ideas or important connections.

  5. In Section 3, follow the directions to rank the relative priority of functions in terms of achieving current sustainability goals and creating value for the company.  Use this framework to choose the highest priority functions to engage in Section 4.  Of course you can engage as many functions as you wish, but this helps prioritize your time and that of others.  

    1. In both categories, score each of the functions listed and their relative importance from 0 (no impact) to 3 (high impact).  Ultimately all functions play a role in company success, but try to think critically about which ones are more important to achieving current goals and company value creation.

  6. In Section 4, there are three questions followed by reflections that seek to understand level of integration for each priority function categorized in terms of

    1. Strategic Integration - How has the function strategically engaged in your company's sustainability efforts?

    2. Operational Integration - How is the function implementing sustainability within its function and responsibilities?

    3. Cultural Integration - How has the unique culture and strengths of the function been leveraged (Doors & Locks) to advance sustainability and corporate success.

            

  1. After all sections have been completed the Self Assessment tool with automatically generate a report.  Automatic graphical output will highlight priority areas pointing to opportunities in the desired state that you chose.

  2. In this prototype UNGC LEAD phase, if you would like to discuss your results with UNGC staff or advisors, please contact UNGC to set up an appointment.
