Structure

The Self-Assessment tool is organized in four sections:

  1. Introduction and Instructions

  2. Readiness Assessment - 5 questions related to the relative development and overall integration of sustainability at the corporate level.

  3. Priority Function Identification - A quick, subjective survey to help identify priority functions for deeper analysis in the last, Functional Assessment section.

  4. Functional Assessment - These questions are designed to be answered through direct discussions between sustainability leaders and peers in the chosen function.






INTRODUCTION

Integrating Sustainability into Business Functions

Increasingly companies with experience in corporate sustainability conclude that deeper integration into their business functions and management processes is necessary to achieve greater levels of value creation, especially when shifting the focus of sustainability beyond risk management and productivity to innovating for growth. Even the best sustainability teams can only take their companies so far by themselves.


For more information on the case for functional sustainability integration see UN Global Compact Briefing Document, Introduction to Functional Integration.


In traditional business terms, organizational integration applies to efforts that seek to continuously align the strategies, practices and policies of autonomous and independent entities to facilitate coordination, collaboration, synergies and greater innovation.


The Roadmap for Integrated Sustainability project seeks to help sustainability teams, or executive leaders responsible for sustainability goals, to engage and integrate sustainability with the company’s priority business functions. The choice of priority functions and the engagement strategies differ depending on the developmental stage of sustainability and its integration in overall business strategy.

The Roadmap Sustainability Stages Model

Though every company’s sustainability journey is unique, many companies evolve through similar stages with similar characteristics, priorities and opportunities to create value.

Underpinning the Roadmap for Integrated Sustainability project, and this Functional Integration Self-Assessment tool, is a Sustainability Stages model, which represents a typical journey of sustainability integration in five general stages. Progressing through the stages, the model describes how companies generally move from a mostly reactive approach to sustainability aimed at protecting the brand and reducing risks to a more proactive and strategic approach, where sustainability becomes a source of resource optimization and value creation.

  1. Stage 1 - Crisis Management - The company’s license to operate is threatened by legal enforcement actions or public outcry.

  2. Stage 2 - Compliance - The company manages its liabilities and risk by ensuring compliance with the law and all labour, environmental, health and safety regulations.

  3. Stage 3 - Resource Optimization - The company moves beyond compliance and risk to reducing costs and increasing productivity.

  4. Stage 4 - Market Differentiation - The company shifts to sustainability-driven innovation, differentiation and growth.

  5. Stage 5 - Purpose-Driven - In this, the highest stage of sustainability integration currently imagined, sustainability issues have become inseparable from the core vision, mission and brand(s) of the company presenting opportunities to transform the company’s business models and role in society.

For more information on the development of corporate sustainability see the UN Global Compact Briefing Document, Sustainability Stages Model.



Priority Functions

Ultimately the goal of leading companies is to integrate sustainability fully across their entire companies and in all stakeholder relationships. But along the journey, different functions have different roles and priorities. This Self Assessment has a quick process to help companies prioritize the engagement of functions based on two subjective criteria:

  1. What functions are most important to drive progress toward current sustainability goals?

  2. What functions are perceived to be particularly influential in the company’s core strategy and value creation?

Following the instructions in the “Priority Function Identification Section” will help companies focus their efforts on the top 4-6 functions to explore more deeply in peer-to-peer based function specific conversations in the “Functional Assessment” section. This is not meant to limit integration discussions. Opportunities can be found in any function. Companies are welcome to schedule conversations to discuss the Functional Assessment questions with peers in as many of the functions as they wish.


For more information on establishing priority targets for functional integration see UN Global Compact Briefing Document, Priority Functions for Sustainability.




The 3 Lenses of Integrated Sustainability


The process of engagement and integration is characterized in three separate but interrelated perspectives or lenses: Strategic, Operational and Cultural Integration. Each of these lenses will be explored for each function. For more information, see the Global Compact Briefing Document, Three Lenses to Integration.

  • Strategic Integration - Incorporating sustainability into the core strategy of an organization (enterprise, business unit, or function). In this context, “strategy” is used to describe the deliberate or emergent choices (purpose, priorities, and goals) that are made to distinctively position the enterprise, brand or product for “sustainable” (enduring) competitive advantage and value creation. It’s about “Doing the right things.” For more information see the UN Global Compact Briefing Document, Strategic Integration.

  • Operational Integration - Executing and reviewing the performance of sustainability related strategies in the regular operating routine of an organization. This lens describes the processes, policies and practices put in place to improve the execution of strategy or sometimes called operational effectiveness. It’s about “Doing things right.” For more information see the UN Global Compact Briefing Document, Operational Integration.

  • Cultural Integration - Leveraging the unique identity, strengths, values, and relationships of an existing organization to advance sustainability and corporate success. To achieve a business’s potential, strategic and operational integration must consider the unique perspectives and skills culture, identity and strengths of organizations and their components. Culture can be a significant barrier or powerful amplifier of executing strategy. For more information see the UN Global Compact Briefing Document, Cultural Integration.







ASSESSMENT TOOL INSTRUCTIONS

  1. The tool is designed to be lead by the Sustainability Function completing the Readiness and Priority Function sections by itself (no more than 30 minutes). The Functional Assessment section is designed to be completed in dialog with functional peers (approximately 30-45 minutes each).

  2. For each question in the Readiness and Functional Assessment sections, please indicate your best assessment of your sustainability program on three dimensions:

    1. Current Performance: For each question, pick the level of performance (1 to 5) that best characterizes your current state. While you will likely have examples of programs described by multiple answers, choose the selection that describes the “center of gravity” of your current program.

    2. Target Performance: Indicate the level of performance you would like to achieve in 2-4 years. (same scale, 1 to 5)

    3. Criticality: Indicate how critical this capability is to driving business value through sustainability (Low, Medium, or High)

    4. Comments are optional, but highly encouraged to capture new ideas or important connections for further internal review or in conversation with advisors at UNGC.

  1. Priority Function Identification - In this section, follow the directions to rank the relative priority of functions in terms of achieving current sustainability goals and creating value for the company. Use this framework to choose the highest priority functions to engage in Functional Assessment section. Of course you can engage as many functions as you wish, but this helps prioritize your time and that of others.

    1. In both categories, score each of the functions listed with their relative importance from 0 (no impact) to 3 (high impact). Ultimately all functions play a role in company success, but try to think critically about which ones are more important to achieving current goals and core business strategy and value creation.

  2. Functional Assessment - In this section, there are three questions that seek to understand the current and desired level of integration for each priority function in terms of

    1. Integration Lenses

      • Strategic Integration - How has the function strategically engaged in your company's sustainability efforts?

      • Operational Integration - How is the function implementing sustainability within its function and responsibilities?

      • Cultural Integration - How has the unique culture and strengths of the function been leveraged (Doors & Locks) to advance sustainability and corporate success.

    2. Reflection Questions - Important - The objective of this self assessment is to uncover new opportunities or priorities to more deeply integrate sustainability into priority functions for mutual benefit. Please schedule sufficient time to discuss these questions and capture responses in your peer dialog sessions.



UNDERSTANDING YOUR AUTOMATED REPORT

Automated Report - After the survey is completed, the Self Assessment tool will automatically generate a report. Remember, the survey will not allow you to “Conclude” unless all the Readiness and Priority Function Identification questions have been answered, as well as at least three Functional Assessments.

  1. Assessment Overview - This histogram presents a graphical picture of how you described the current state of sustainability integration at your company. Boxes are labeled by Readiness question and Function. Functional boxes are color coded by Integration Lens.

    1. Overall shape - The overall shape of the histogram reflects the distribution and “center of gravity” of the development of your sustainability initiatives plotted on the UNGC Sustainability Stages Model. It could be interesting to compare that shape with your chosen Sustainability Stage (box labeled, SOC).

    2. Readiness - The four readiness questions are shown in red boxes. If these boxes lag most of the rest of the responses (i.e., appear on the left side or slope of the histogram shape) they could be creating significant barriers to integration efforts. Consider investing in these corporate wide issues prior to major functional integration efforts.

    3. Functional Integration - Individual functional lenses are plotted on the histogram. Pay particular attention to those lagging lenses that could be holding back the progress of a function and potentially your overall sustainability program.

  2. Functional Snapshot - This radar (spider) diagram displays the current state of the 3 integration lenses for each priority function assessed and its relative priority.

    1. Priority - The relative priority is estimated by combining the average gap between current and desired level and the expressed business criticality of that function. The higher ratings (furthest from center) suggest the functions of higher potential impact and therefore priority for your integration efforts.

    2. Lenses - The current state for each lens of the reported function is plotted. While significant opportunities may exist within each of the higher priority functions, pay particular attention to the lagging lenses (lowest scores, nearer to center). These areas may be creating significant barriers to progress affecting that function and perhaps others.

  3. Detailed Output - The remaining sections report the answers chosen and documented comments for each question in the survey.

    1. Stage Selections - For both the Readiness and Functional Assessment sections, the contents of the light blue boxes are copied from the generalized “answers” that you chose in the survey for both the current and desired levels for that question. The “answers” were constructed to not only help you choose the relative “center of gravity” of your existing program for that function and lens, but also to suggest generalized areas of best practice exhibited by companies already well positioned at your desired level.

    2. The medium blue boxes contain any personalized comments you captured for opportunities and next steps. In addition, in the upper right, a relative priority is suggested for that topic area. When determining potential opportunities and next steps, consider both your personalized comments and the generalized best practice opportunities from the desired level for that question.

    3. Functional Priority - The report includes a graphical representation of your relative influence responses in the Priority Function Identification section. As a reminder, it is increasingly important to strategically engage with those functions that are perceived to be particularly influential in the company’s core strategy and value creation, especially when adopting a more growth oriented sustainability strategy, i.e. moving beyond a “Resource Optimization” focus to shift to “Market Differentiation.”
